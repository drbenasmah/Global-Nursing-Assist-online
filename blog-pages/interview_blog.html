How to Ace Your Nursing Interview in Malta

Dr. Ben Asmah August 26, 2025 · 5 min read

Landing a nursing job in Malta is exciting—but the interview can be intimidating. What separates successful candidates from those who struggle often comes down to preparation, strategy, and confidence. Maltese healthcare employers are looking for nurses who demonstrate not only strong clinical knowledge but also professionalism, patient safety awareness, and ethical judgment.

This guide walks you through the essential areas to focus on so you can make a confident impression and secure your dream role.

Step 1: Build Your Bedrock of Clinical Knowledge 🩺

Before anything else, your clinical knowledge forms the backbone of your interview performance. Maltese interviewers often present clinical scenarios to test whether you can apply safe and effective nursing practice. Be prepared to discuss:

  • Patient Assessment – Core procedures like monitoring vital signs, conducting head-to-toe assessments, and performing neurological checks using the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS).
  • Medication Safety – The “Five Rights” of medication administration are essential. Be ready to explain how you administer safely, handle errors, and educate patients.
  • Procedural Skills – Common tasks such as IV line insertion, urinary catheterisation, and central line care. Always emphasise aseptic technique.
  • Emergency Response – How you would manage a patient in respiratory distress, respond to a cardiac arrest, or recognise early signs of sepsis.
💡

Top Tip: Don’t just list steps. Explain the why behind each action. For example, when describing IV insertion, note that you release the tourniquet after flashback to prevent vein damage. This demonstrates deeper understanding, not just rote memorisation.

Step 2: Demonstrate Your Professional Character and Ethics 🤝

Technical skill is only part of the picture. Maltese employers also assess whether you can act as a safe, ethical professional. Be ready to discuss:

  • Teamwork and Communication – Show clear and safe handovers using structured tools like SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation).
  • Ethical Principles – Apply autonomy, beneficence, and non-maleficence to real clinical scenarios.
  • Professional Development – Highlight your commitment to lifelong learning through nursing journals, workshops, or quality improvement projects.

Step 3: Master Behavioral Questions with the STAR Method ⭐

Behavioral questions test how you’ve acted in past situations. Using the STAR Method keeps your answers structured and impactful:

  • Situation: Describe the context.
  • Task: What was your responsibility?
  • Action: What steps did you take?
  • Result: What was the outcome?

Example Question: “Describe a time you had to deal with a difficult patient or family member.”

“I was caring for an elderly patient whose family felt anxious and uninformed about the care plan. My goal was to de-escalate the situation and build trust. I scheduled a brief meeting, listened actively, explained the care plan in simple terms, and provided regular updates. As a result, the family became reassured and collaborative, which improved the patient’s care experience.”
💡

Pro Tip: Practicing STAR answers in advance ensures you sound confident, not rehearsed.

Step 4: Sample Malta Interview Questions with Model Answers

Question 1 – How would you handle a medication error?

“During a busy shift, I noticed a colleague about to administer the wrong dosage. My responsibility was to ensure patient safety and follow hospital protocol. I politely stopped the process, we double-checked the patient chart and prescription together, and I ensured the error was reported according to policy. The patient received the correct medication safely, and the incident contributed to team learning on preventing future errors.”
💡

Key Point: Always highlight patient safety first and adherence to hospital protocol.

Question 2 – Why is infection control important in a hospital environment?

"Infection control is essential to protect patients, colleagues, and the wider community. Many hospitalized patients are vulnerable, so even a small lapse can cause serious infections. By rigorously following protocols—hand hygiene, PPE use, and isolation measures—nurses prevent harm, improve outcomes, and uphold our professional responsibility to do no harm."
💡

Key Point: Keep answers patient-centered and safety-focused.
